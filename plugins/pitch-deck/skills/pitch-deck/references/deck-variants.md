# Deck Variants — Reference

Per-variant adjustments to the standard 10-slide arc. Picked in Phase 1 Step 2 of
SKILL.md. The standard arc stays intact — these adjustments tune slide depth, order,
and the ask — they don't introduce new slide types.

The four variants:

1. **Cold-email send** — attached to a first-touch email to an investor who's never
   heard of you.
2. **Warm-intro meeting** — you have 20–45 minutes with a VC partner who already
   has context.
3. **Demo day** — 3–5 minutes on stage in front of hundreds, followed by 1:1s.
4. **Follow-up after a first meeting** — sent to an investor who asked follow-up
   questions or wants to share internally.

---

## Variant 1 — Cold Email Send

**Audience reads it alone, probably on a phone, probably in under 2 minutes.**

### Tuning

| Slide | Adjustment |
|-------|------------|
| Title | Tagline must be concrete enough that a stranger understands it. "AI for dentists" fails; "Scheduling software for single-location dental practices" works. |
| Problem | Lead with the specific customer quote. No setup, no context. Investor is skimming. |
| Solution | One sentence, one screenshot if possible — no wall of text. |
| Market | Keep TAM/SAM/SOM but tighten the bottom-up to 3 lines. |
| Product | One hero screenshot, 2 callouts max. |
| Business Model | Price band + gross margin if known. Omit if pre-revenue. |
| Traction | This is where cold-email decks win or lose. Strongest chart + 2 named logos / customer quotes. If pre-revenue, waitlist growth curve or LOIs. |
| Team | Faces + 1 line per founder — no multi-paragraph bios. |
| Competition | 2×2 positioning, nothing longer. |
| Ask | Amount, 3 milestones, runway. Name the lead if you have one — cold-email decks with "oversubscribed" or a named lead get 10× the response rate. |

### Length: aim for 10 slides, hard cap 12.

### What to add: none. A cold-email deck is not the place to add Why Now or Vision slides — they read as padding.

### What to cut: the Appendix. Investors who want more will reply.

### One rule: assume the investor reads in under 2 minutes and stops when they lose interest. **Slide 3 must earn slide 4.**

## Variant 2 — Warm-Intro Meeting

**Audience reads it with you in the room, 20–45 minutes, asking questions.**

### Tuning

| Slide | Adjustment |
|-------|------------|
| Title | Less important — you're already in the meeting. |
| Problem | Can be slightly longer, lead with a customer story (60–90 seconds spoken). |
| Solution | Can expand to 2 slides (what it does + the key insight that makes it work) if the insight is defensible and interesting. |
| Market | Full TAM/SAM/SOM with sources. Expect to be pushed on sources. |
| Product | 2–3 screenshots or a short demo. This is the slide most likely to get into a 5-minute tangent — plan for it. |
| Business Model | Full detail: ACV, contract length, gross margin, sales cycle, churn (if known). |
| Traction | Can expand to 2 slides if you have both revenue and logos worth showing. Cohort retention belongs here or in appendix. |
| Team | Expand: why-this-team-for-this-problem story per founder, key advisors, critical open roles. |
| Competition | 2×2 + brief verbal tour of each competitor. Be honest about where they're stronger — it builds trust. |
| Ask | Full ask with milestones tied to hiring plan + runway math. Be ready to name the terms you want. |

### Length: 12–18 slides is fine. Appendix optional.

### What to add: Why Now slide if the timing story is strong. Vision slide if your year-5 story genuinely opens new markets.

### What to cut: nothing structural — but cut any slide where you can't field a follow-up question. Investors remember the slide where you stumbled.

### One rule: the deck is the scaffold, not the script. **Plan for interruption.** If you can't take a detour on slide 4 and come back, the deck is too rigid.

## Variant 3 — Demo Day

**Audience: 200–2000 people, many investors, 3–5 minutes on stage.**

### Tuning

| Slide | Adjustment |
|-------|------------|
| Title | Bold, logo-forward, name + tagline large enough to read from the back of a room. |
| Problem | 30 seconds. One customer, one pain, one number. |
| Solution | 30 seconds. One sentence, one screenshot. |
| Market | One slide, TAM/SAM/SOM in a single chart. Bottom-up in speaker notes, not on slide. |
| Product | Live demo if possible — if not, animated GIF. Static screenshots feel flat on stage. |
| Business Model | One line. "$X ACV, annual subscription, Y% gross margin." |
| Traction | The money slide. One chart, big, one trend line, one number. This is what gets remembered. |
| Team | Faces larger than in a sit-down deck. One line per founder — "10 years at Google, shipped Gmail's spam filter" is fine. |
| Competition | 2×2 or a single sentence: "Unlike X, we do Y." Skip the feature table. |
| Ask | Specific amount, named lead if committed, contact info on the final slide. |

### Length: 8–10 slides, hard cap 12. You have 3–5 minutes.

### What to add: a memorable visual on the closing slide (tagline + QR to contact) — demo day decks are remembered by the last slide shown.

### What to cut: Why Now (goes in pitch script, not deck), Vision (belongs in 1:1 follow-ups), Appendix.

### One rule: a demo day deck is 80% spoken delivery, 20% slides. **The deck supports the pitch; it doesn't replace it.** Slides should be scannable from 30 feet away.

## Variant 4 — Follow-Up After First Meeting

**Audience: the investor you just met, plus the partners they're about to share with.**

### Tuning

| Slide | Adjustment |
|-------|------------|
| Title | Same as cold-email variant. |
| Problem → Ask | Same 10-slide arc, but **incorporate the questions the investor actually asked.** If they drilled on churn, add a cohort retention slide. If they questioned TAM, rework the market slide to address their specific concern. |
| Appendix | Strongly encourage an appendix for this variant. This is where cohort data, unit economics, detailed customer list, and GTM plan go. The partner the investor shares with will scan the appendix. |

### Adjustments unique to this variant

- **Prepend a "Since we last spoke" slide (or note in the cover email).** List 2–3
  concrete updates since the meeting — new customer, new hire, new metric. Shows
  momentum; partners love reading it.
- **Add a "Questions you asked" slide** near the back if the investor had multiple
  deep questions. Answer them crisply, on the record. This is a trust move.
- **If the investor requested specific data, it goes in the main deck, not the
  appendix.** Moving their question to the appendix reads as evasion.

### Length: 12–18 slides + appendix. Appendix can be 10+ slides for this variant.

### What to add: the updates slide, the questions-answered slide, the appendix.

### What to cut: nothing — this is the one variant where longer is fine.

### One rule: the partner who wasn't in the first meeting is the actual audience. **Write for them.** They'll skim, scan the appendix, and form an opinion in under 5 minutes without ever having met you.

## Applying the variant

The variant choice in Phase 1 Step 2 shapes:

- **Slide count and depth** — which slides expand, which compress
- **Which optional slides are worth adding** (Why Now / Vision / Appendix)
- **Which anti-patterns are more dangerous for this audience** (TAM-only kills cold
  emails faster than warm intros; traction-without-time-axis kills demo-day decks
  because the audience has no time to squint)
- **What the closing slide should emphasize** (contact info for cold and demo day;
  next-step ask for warm intros; appendix pointer for follow-ups)

If the founder isn't sure which variant, default to **Warm-Intro Meeting** — it's
the most flexible and the easiest to cut down into the other three.

---

## What does NOT change across variants

- The four cardinal sins are cardinal in all variants (TAM-only, traction without
  time axis, team without faces/experience, vague ask). None of the variants lets
  the deck ship without them.
- The standard 10-slide arc order — problem before solution, product before business
  model, traction before team. Never reorder to "open with traction" even if traction
  is the strongest slide; the narrative depends on the pain landing first.
- The one-claim-per-slide rule.
- The 90-second read test (see `deck-checklist.md`).
