# Exemplars — what good looks like, and the honesty caveat

Reference material, not rules. The point of this file is calibration: the best
gamification is **invisible as gamification** — it reads as good product design.
Cheap gamification is loud, bolted-on, and extrinsic. Train the eye on the
difference.

---

## Good (often invisible) — study these

- **The contribution graph** (GitHub-style). A calendar heatmap of real activity.
  No points, no competition — just your own work, made visible. Feeds *competence*
  and *autonomy*; it's a record you built, not a leash. The archetypal Group A
  mechanic.
- **The satisfying "done" state.** A crisp completion moment — the checkbox that
  feels good to tick, the task that clears with a clean animation. Reinforces
  finishing without any external currency.
- **Quiet progress.** A profile-completeness ring, a course progress bar, a
  collection that fills from genuine use. Shows the arc; doesn't manufacture one.
- **Mastery feedback.** A typing tutor showing words-per-minute climbing; a
  language app surfacing *which* skill improved. Specific competence signal, tied to
  real skill — not a generic "+10 points."
- **Personal best, not social rank.** "Your longest run yet" beats "you're #347 of
  10,000." Same data, opposite emotional effect on the bottom 80%.
- **Snappy, earned interactions.** Linear-grade responsiveness, a progress fill with
  the right easing. This isn't a "mechanic" at all — it's craft — but it's most of
  what makes gamification *feel* good (see the caveat below).

**Common thread:** each amplifies a motivation the user already had. None of them
work by making *stopping* feel like loss.

---

## Cheap (bolted-on) — recognize and refuse

- **Participation badges.** A badge for logging in, for completing onboarding, for
  existing. Noise that devalues the badges that mark real achievement.
- **Engagement points with no meaning.** A points currency that buys nothing and
  measures nothing the user cares about — pure Skinner-box scaffolding.
- **Manufactured-loss streaks.** "Don't break your 14-day streak!" The reward is now
  *avoiding loss*, not the activity. Corrosive; a veto trip.
- **Demotivating leaderboards.** Social ranking that tells most users they're losing.
- **Confetti everywhere.** Celebration inflation — when everything is a win,
  nothing is. The "done" state stops feeling earned.

---

## The "feel" honesty caveat (carry this into every report)

Good gamification is, more than anything, **textural craft**: the easing curve on a
progress fill, the asymmetric timing of a reward animation, the weight of a
"complete" moment, the millisecond responsiveness that makes an action feel *good*
to repeat. **A static report cannot deliver any of that.**

So the report must be honest about its own limits:

- **Prescribe motion specs + references, not vibes.** "A 600ms ease-out fill with a
  subtle overshoot, like [reference]" — not "make it feel rewarding."
- **State plainly that implementation craft is required**, and that the mechanic is
  only as good as that craft. A perfectly-chosen mechanic shipped with janky timing
  feels worse than no mechanic at all.
- **Never claim the feel.** The report recommends *what* and *why*; the *how it
  feels* is built, not written.

This caveat is rendered (verbatim-equivalent) in every report's Phase 7 output —
the skill's credibility depends on not overselling what a document can do.
